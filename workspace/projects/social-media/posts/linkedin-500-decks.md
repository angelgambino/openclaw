## "What I Learned Reviewing 500 Pitch Decks in 20 Years"

---

I've reviewed over 500 pitch decks in 20 years.

The ones that got funded had almost nothing in common with the ones that didn't.

Here's what actually separated them:

1. The best decks don't explain the product. They make the market feel inevitable.

2. If your TAM slide needs defending, your market isn't big enough.

3. Founders who lead with traction close faster than founders who lead with vision. Vision without numbers is a TED talk.

4. The "why now" slide is where most decks die. If you can't make me feel urgency, I'm already looking at the next one.

5. Nobody funds a team slide with headshots and bios. They fund a team slide that answers: "Why is THIS group the only group that can win THIS market?"

6. Your competitive landscape slide is a tell. Matrix with you in the top right corner? You don't understand your competitors.

7. The ask slide should be one sentence. Amount. Use of funds. Timeline to next milestone. That's it.

Most founders over-design and under-think.

The deck isn't the product. It's the argument.

If your deck isn't converting, it's not a design problem — it's a strategy problem.
