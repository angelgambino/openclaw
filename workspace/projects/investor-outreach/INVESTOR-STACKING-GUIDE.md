# Investor Stacking Guide

**How to Build a Round Using the Master Investor Database**

---

## 1. What Is Round Stacking?

Building a fundraising round is not random — it's a deliberate sequence. You **stack** investors in a specific order to manufacture momentum, social proof, and urgency.

Here's the core insight most first-time founders miss: **every investor you close makes the next one easier to close.** A seed round isn't one negotiation — it's a chain reaction. The first check is the hardest. The last check practically closes itself.

Round stacking is the discipline of choosing *who to approach, in what order, and why* so that each commitment compounds into the next. Done right, you go from "we're raising" to "round is oversubscribed" in 8-10 weeks. Done wrong, you spend 6 months in fundraising purgatory with a half-full round and decaying momentum.

**The psychology:** Investors are herd animals. They don't want to be first (too risky) and they don't want to miss out (FOMO). Your job is to engineer a sequence where each layer of investors creates just enough signal to pull in the next layer.

## 2. The 4-Layer Stack

Every well-built round has four layers, approached in order. Each layer serves a specific strategic function.

### Layer 1: Anchor Angels (Approach First — Creates Signal)

| Attribute | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Who** | Solo angels who decide fast (1-7 days), write personal checks |
| **Typical Check Size** | $25K–$100K |
| **Why first** | Fast commits create momentum. Other investors hear "deal is moving." |
| **Database filter** | `Speed of Close` = Fast/Very Fast + `Type of Investor` contains "Angel Investor" + `Typical Check Size` = "$25K-$100K" |

**What to look for in the database:**

- Filter `Speed of Close` for "Fast" — these investors don't need committee approval
- Filter `Type of Investor` for "Angel Investor" or "Solo Angel" — solo decision-makers
- Check `Typical Check Size` = "$25K-$100K" — small enough to commit without deep diligence
- Prioritize investors with `Relationship Strength` ≥ Warm — you need fast yeses, not cold outreach

**Example investors from the database:**

- **Sahil Lavingia** (Gumroad founder) — creator economy angel, writes fast checks
- **Naval Ravikant** (AngelList) — prolific angel, massive signal value
- **Elad Gil** — operator-angel, known for quick decisions on conviction bets
- **Eric Bahn** (Hustle Fund) — micro-VC with angel-speed decision-making
- **Angel Gambino** — multi-thesis angel (creator economy, mental health, AI, music), appears across 6/6 enrichment lists in the database

**Your goal for Layer 1:** 3-5 angel commits totaling $150K-$500K within the first 2 weeks.

**The pitch at this stage:** You're not selling the round — you're selling the *opportunity to be early*. "I'm raising a seed. You'd be one of the first checks in. Here's why this is going to move fast."

### Layer 2: Strategic Angels (Approach Second — Adds Credibility)

| Attribute | Detail |
|---|---|

| **Typical Check Size** | $50K–$250K |

**What to look for in the database:**

- Cross-reference with `Priority Tier` = High and `Thesis Match Score` ≥ 4 — these investors deeply understand your space
- Check `Why This Investor is a Fit` — the database explains exactly why each investor maps to your thesis
- Look at `Suggested Angle` — use this to tailor your pitch to what resonates with them

**Example investors from the database (by sector):**

**Your goal for Layer 2:** 2-4 strategic angels adding $200K-$500K, committed by week 3-4.

**The pitch at this stage:** Name-drop your Layer 1 commits. "We have [Anchor Angel] and [Anchor Angel] already in. Given your background in [their sector], you'd be a perfect strategic addition to the cap table."

### Layer 3: Lead Investor (Approach Third — Sets Terms)

| Attribute | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Who** | Seed-stage VCs who lead rounds, set terms, and often take board seats |
| **Typical Check Size** | $500K–$2M |
| **Why third** | By now you have momentum + signal. The lead sees 3-5 angels already committed and thinks "this founder can execute." |
| **Database filter** | `Leads Rounds?` = Yes + `Stage: Investment Thesis` = Seed + `Type of Investor` contains "VC" or "Micro-VC" |

**What to look for in the database:**

- Filter `Leads Rounds?` = "Yes" — this is the single most important filter for Layer 3
- Check `Stage: Investment Thesis` includes "Seed" — confirm they invest at your stage
- Look at `Typical Check Size` for $500K+ — leads write the biggest check
- Review `Likelihood to Invest` and `Thesis Match Score` — prioritize investors scoring 4-5
- Check `Warm Intro Path` — warm intros are *essential* for leads. Cold outreach to lead VCs has a <2% conversion rate

**Example lead investors from the database:**

- **Benchmark** — top-tier seed, sets terms, high signal
- **First Round Capital** — classic seed lead, strong brand
- **Floodgate** — early seed specialist
- **Hustle Fund** — founder-friendly, fast process
- **Pear VC** — pre-seed/seed focus, known for leading
- **Slow Ventures** (Dave Morin) — appeared on 5/6 enrichment lists, strong multi-thesis fit

**Your goal for Layer 3:** Secure 1 lead VC committing $500K-$2M by week 4-6.

**The pitch at this stage:** Lead with committed capital. "We've closed $500K+ from [names]. [Strategic Angel with domain expertise] validated our approach to [problem]. We're looking for a lead to close out a [$X] round at [terms]."

**Critical:** The lead sets the valuation and terms. Everything before this point should be on a SAFE or convertible note so you're not negotiating price with 5 different angels.

### Layer 4: Fill (Approach Last — Close the Round)

| Attribute | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Who** | Syndicates, family offices, following VCs, fund-of-funds |
| **Typical Check Size** | $100K–$500K |
| **Why last** | Once the lead is in, these investors follow. Minimal selling required. |
| **Database filter** | `Leads Rounds?` = No or Sometimes + `Type of Investor` contains "Syndicate," "Family Office," or "VC" with smaller check sizes |

**What to look for in the database:**

- Filter `Leads Rounds?` = "No" or "Sometimes" — these are natural followers
- Look at `Type of Investor` for "Syndicate," "Family Office," "Limited Partner Investor"
- Check the `Fund` column — some funds specifically exist to fill rounds alongside known leads
- Cross-reference your lead's known syndication partners — if your lead typically co-invests with certain funds, approach those funds for Layer 4

**Your goal for Layer 4:** Fill the remaining allocation ($500K-$750K) within 2-3 weeks of securing the lead.

**The pitch at this stage:** This practically writes itself. "[Lead VC] is leading our [$X] round. We have $[Y] remaining. Interested in the allocation?"

## 3. The Sequencing Playbook

Here's the week-by-week execution plan:

### Week 1-2: Anchor Angels (Layer 1)

- [ ] Filter database: `Speed of Close` = Fast + `Type of Investor` = Angel + `Typical Check Size` = $25K-$100K
- [ ] Identify 10-15 target angels; rank by `Relationship Strength` and `Likelihood to Invest`
- [ ] Reach out to top 10 simultaneously (angels expect parallel processes)
- [ ] Use `Suggested Angle` from database to customize each pitch
- [ ] **Target:** 3-5 signed SAFEs, $150K-$500K committed
- [ ] Update `Status` column in database as you go (Not Contacted → Contacted → Committed)

### Week 3-4: Strategic Angels (Layer 2)

- [ ] Filter database: `Sector: Investment Thesis` matches your vertical + `Priority Tier` = High + `Thesis Match Score` ≥ 4
- [ ] Approach 5-8 strategic angels with "round is filling" framing
- [ ] Name-drop Layer 1 commits (with permission)
- [ ] Check `Warm Intro Path` — get intros from your Layer 1 angels where possible
- [ ] **Target:** 2-4 additional commits, $200K-$500K added
- [ ] You should now have $400K-$1M committed

### Week 4-6: Lead Investor (Layer 3)

- [ ] Filter database: `Leads Rounds?` = Yes + `Stage: Investment Thesis` = Seed
- [ ] Prioritize by `Thesis Match Score` and `Warm Intro Path` quality
- [ ] Pitch 3-5 potential leads simultaneously (never run a sequential VC process)
- [ ] Lead with "$500K+ already committed from [impressive names]"
- [ ] Review `Likelihood to Invest` — don't waste limited lead-pitch bandwidth on long shots
- [ ] **Target:** 1 lead term sheet, $500K-$2M committed
- [ ] Negotiate terms, sign term sheet

### Week 6-8: Fill (Layer 4)

- [ ] Filter database: `Leads Rounds?` = No/Sometimes + check sizes $100K-$500K
- [ ] Approach investors who co-invest with your lead (check fund networks)
- [ ] Leverage lead's brand: "[Lead VC] is leading — we have [$X] allocation remaining"
- [ ] **Target:** Fill remaining round in 2-3 weeks

### Week 8-10: Close

- [ ] Final legal (note/SAFE conversions, priced round docs if applicable)
- [ ] Wire instructions sent
- [ ] Money in bank
- [ ] Announce (blog post, Twitter/X, press if warranted)
- [ ] Update all investor statuses in database to "Closed"

## 4. How to Use the Database for Stacking

The master investor database (~12,000 investors) has specific columns designed for round-stacking decisions. Here's how to use each one:

### Finding Your Layer 1 (Anchor Angels)

```
Filter: Speed of Close = "Fast"
   AND: Type of Investor CONTAINS "Angel"
   AND: Typical Check Size = "$25K-$100K"
Sort by: Relationship Strength (descending), then Likelihood to Invest
```

These are investors who decide quickly, write personal checks, and don't need partnership meetings. They're your starting lineup.

### Finding Your Layer 2 (Strategic Angels)

```
Filter: Sector: Investment Thesis CONTAINS [your sector]
   AND: Priority Tier = "High"
   AND: Thesis Match Score ≥ 4
Sort by: Thesis Match Score (descending)
```

Cross-check the `Why This Investor is a Fit` column — it explains exactly how each investor connects to your thesis. Use `Suggested Angle` to craft your pitch.

### Finding Your Layer 3 (Lead Investor)

```
Filter: Leads Rounds? = "Yes"
   AND: Stage: Investment Thesis CONTAINS "Seed"
Sort by: Thesis Match Score (descending), then Likelihood to Invest
```

**Critical column:** `Warm Intro Path` — for lead VCs, warm intros are non-negotiable. If this column is empty and `Relationship Strength` = None, deprioritize unless you have another path in.

### Finding Your Layer 4 (Fill)

```
Filter: Leads Rounds? = "No" OR "Sometimes"
   AND: Typical Check Size = "$100K-$500K"
```

Look at fund names and investor networks. If your lead VC has known co-investment patterns, search the `Fund` column for those partners.

### Cross-Cutting Filters

| Database Column | What It Tells You | How to Use It |
|---|---|---|
| `Speed of Close` | How fast they decide | Layer 1 = Fast; Layer 3 = accept slower |
| `Leads Rounds?` | Whether they set terms | Yes = Layer 3; No = Layer 4 |
| `Typical Check Size` | How much they write | Match to your layer expectations |
| `Thesis Match Score` | How well they fit your thesis (1-5) | Prioritize 4-5 across all layers |
| `Priority Tier` | Overall priority ranking | High = approach first within each layer |
| `Sector: Investment Thesis` | What verticals they invest in | Layer 2 strategic fit |
| `Suggested Angle` | How to pitch them | Customize every outreach |
| `Warm Intro Path` | Who can introduce you | Essential for Layer 3, helpful everywhere |
| `Relationship Strength` | Current relationship status | Warm/Strong = faster close |
| `Likelihood to Invest` | Estimated conversion probability | Triage within each layer |
| `Why This Investor is a Fit` | Detailed thesis alignment | Personalize your pitch |
| `Status` | Pipeline tracking | Track progress through the stack |
| `Type of Investor` | Angel, VC, Syndicate, Family Office, etc. | Map to correct layer |

## 5. Common Stacking Mistakes

### ❌ Mistake 1: Approaching Lead VCs First

**What happens:** You pitch Benchmark with zero committed capital. They say "interesting, keep us posted." Translation: pass.

**Why it fails:** Lead VCs want to see signal. No angels committed = no signal = no urgency. You've burned your one shot with that partner.

**Fix:** Never approach a lead VC until you have $300K+ committed from angels. Use the database's `Leads Rounds?` = Yes filter to identify leads — and keep them in Layer 3, not Layer 1.

### ❌ Mistake 2: Too Many Angels Before the Lead

**What happens:** You're so good at closing angels that you raise $800K on SAFEs before finding a lead. Now you have 15 people on your cap table and the lead wants to know why you need them.

**Why it fails:** Leads want meaningful ownership. If 60% of the round is gone, the lead's check isn't large enough to justify a board seat or the time investment.

**Fix:** Cap angel allocation at 25-30% of your target round. For a $2M round, that's $500K-$600K max from angels (Layers 1-2) before closing the lead.

### ❌ Mistake 3: No Urgency

**What happens:** You tell investors "we're raising, no rush, round is open." They say "great, let's reconnect in a few weeks." Weeks turn into months.

**Why it fails:** If there's no deadline, there's no reason to decide. Investors have 100 other deals. Yours goes to the bottom of the pile.

**Fix:** Create real urgency:
- "We're targeting close by [date]"
- "We have [$X] committed and [$Y] in soft commits"
- "Our allocation is getting tight — can you decide by Friday?"
- Track `Status` in the database religiously so you always know your exact committed number

### ❌ Mistake 4: Mixing Incompatible Investors

**What happens:** You get a commit from Fund A, then approach Fund B — not knowing they have a competitive portfolio company or a personal conflict with Fund A's GP.

**Why it fails:** Fund B passes, and now they've seen your deck and data room for nothing. Worse, they might share intel with their portfolio company.

**Fix:** Before approaching any investor, check the `Fund` column and do basic research on portfolio overlap. If your database's `Why This Investor is a Fit` mentions specific portfolio companies, cross-reference for conflicts.

### ❌ Mistake 5: Treating Every Investor the Same

**What happens:** You send the same cold email to an angel and a Tier 1 VC. The angel might reply. The VC definitely won't.

**Why it fails:** Different layers require different approaches, different materials, and different timelines.

**Fix:** Use the `Suggested Angle` and `Warm Intro Path` columns for *every* investor. The database has already done the work of identifying how to approach each one. Use it.
