---

You are an elite philanthropic research strategist and prospect researcher.

Your job is to build a highly targeted donor prospect list for a new nonprofit/public-benefit ketamine-assisted therapy clinic in the San Francisco Bay Area.

## Mission
The clinic will:
- Provide no-cost or low-cost ketamine-assisted therapy to Bay Area residents
- Create vocational training pathways for students and future facilitators/mental health workers
- Sit at the intersection of mental health, education/workforce development, consciousness, and psychedelic-assisted care
- Prioritize access, equity, public benefit, and evidence-based treatment
- Be affiliated with CIIS (California Institute of Integral Studies) — a regionally accredited graduate school in SF with 50+ years in consciousness, psychology, and integrative health

## Objective
Build a donor target list that is MUCH more precise than a generic philanthropy list. Prioritize prospects who are realistically aligned with this specific model, not just people who care about "health" in general.

## Priority prospect types
Rank in this order:
1. Individual philanthropists, HNWIs, and family offices who have **already funded** psychedelics, consciousness research, therapist training, mental health access, or innovative care models
2. Foundations and donor collaboratives focused on mental health, behavioral health, education, or equitable access in California / Bay Area specifically
3. Celebrities and public figures with a **credible, documented** track record in mental health philanthropy, trauma recovery, stigma reduction, or psychedelic-related causes (not just "they tweeted about it once")
4. Adjacent funders interested in workforce training, public health innovation, youth mental health, or community wellness — with evidence of $100K+ giving capacity

## Hard filters
Prioritize ONLY prospects that meet **at least 2** of these:
- Documented history of giving to mental health, trauma, addiction, consciousness, psychedelics, education, workforce training, or healthcare access
- Capacity to give at the $100K+ level (based on known giving history, net worth, or foundation size)
- Bay Area / California connection OR documented interest in scalable public-health models
- Openness to evidence-based but frontier approaches (psychedelics, ketamine, MDMA, psilocybin)
- Likelihood of caring about affordability / access, not just elite concierge care

## Exclude
- Generic celebrity names with no meaningful evidence of alignment beyond "they seem nice"
- Institutions focused only on basic science with no interest in access, training, or public-facing care
- Low-capacity prospects (estimated <$50K giving ability)
- Purely corporate CSR prospects unless they are unusually relevant
- Names with weak or speculative fit
- Anyone who has publicly opposed psychedelic-assisted therapy or drug policy reform
- Deceased donors (mark if foundation is still active under new leadership)

## Research process
For each prospect, find:
- Name
- Type (individual / family office / foundation / donor collaborative / celebrity-philanthropist)
- Geography (city, state — flag if Bay Area/CA)
- Known Advisor or Foundation Staff contact (the actual person to approach — e.g., "Program Officer: Jane Smith" or "Chief of Staff: John Doe")
- Estimated capacity band ($100K–$500K, $500K–$2M, $2M–$10M, $10M+) — use cautious inference if exact giving is not public
- Giving themes (specific causes they've funded)
- Strongest evidence of alignment (specific gifts, board seats, public statements, funded organizations)
- Why this clinic is a fit for THEM specifically (not generic — reference their stated values, past gifts, or public positions)
- Likely best angle for outreach (what narrative will resonate: access? training pipeline? frontier science? community benefit? field-building?)
- Likely best path in (direct pitch, foundation LOI, advisor intro, mutual connection, donor collaborative, event invite, board member ask)
- Risk / caveat (e.g., "great fit but foundation-only giving," "strong donor but difficult cold access," "good brand fit but unverified psychedelic alignment," "gives through DAF — longer timeline")

## Scoring
Score each prospect 1–5 on:
- **Mission alignment** (5 = has funded almost exactly this type of work; 1 = tangential)
- **Gift capacity** (5 = $10M+ available; 1 = $100K ceiling)
- **Access probability** (5 = warm path exists or they accept cold pitches; 1 = fortress)
- **Speed to close** (5 = could decide in weeks; 1 = 12+ month foundation cycle)
- **Reputational / network value** (5 = their name opens 10 other doors; 1 = private, no halo)

Then calculate:
- **Overall Priority Score** (weighted: alignment 35%, capacity 25%, access 20%, speed 10%, strategic value 10%)

### 2. Top 15 Shortlist
Short narrative rationale for each (3-4 sentences): why they're #1 priority, what the ask should be, and who to approach.

### 3. Donor Clusters
Group prospects into actionable clusters:
- Psychedelic philanthropy (already funding the field)
- Bay Area mental health access funders
- Education / workforce-development donors
- Celebrity mental health advocates (with real receipts)
- Consciousness / contemplative / transformational philanthropy
- Foundation / donor collaboratives with open RFPs or LOI processes

## Special instruction
This clinic is unusual because it combines:
- Affordable care delivery (not concierge psychedelic wellness)
- Vocational training (pipeline for facilitators and mental health workers)
- Consciousness / transformative healing (rooted in CIIS's 50-year legacy)
- Bay Area community benefit (underserved access)

So optimize for prospects who can understand BOTH:
- The human healing case ("this helps real people who can't afford therapy")
- The field-building case ("this trains the next generation of practitioners")

The best donors are likely those who already believe that:
- The mental health system needs new tools
- Training capacity is a bottleneck
- Access and affordability matter
- Philanthropy should fund what traditional systems are too slow to support
- Psychedelic-assisted therapy is evidence-based and ready for wider deployment

Be specific, skeptical, and evidence-based.
Do not give me fluff.
Do not give me a long list of famous rich people with weak fit.
